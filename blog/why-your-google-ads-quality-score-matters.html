Why Your Google Ads Quality Score Matters (And How to Improve It)

Quality Score sits quietly next to your keywords, from 1 to 10, and most people ignore it. That's a mistake. It directly affects what you pay per click and where your ads show up, which means it can be the difference between a competitor beating you on price even when your bid is higher.

What Quality Score actually is

Quality Score is Google's way of measuring how relevant and useful your ad is to the person searching. It looks at your keyword, your ad, and the page someone lands on after they click, then scores how well those three things work together.

It matters because Google doesn't just rank ads by who bids the most. It rewards ads that are more useful to searchers with cheaper clicks and better positions. So two advertisers can bid the exact same amount, and the one with the higher Quality Score will beat the other one and pay less to do it.

The three things that make up your score

Quality Score is built from three components. Each one is rated low, average, or above average.

Expected click through rate

This is Google's prediction of how likely people are to click your ad when it shows up for a given keyword. It's based on how your ad has performed for that keyword in the past, not your account as a whole.

Ad relevance

This measures how closely your ad copy matches the keyword someone searched. If your keyword is "emergency plumber Leeds" and your ad talks about plumbing services in general, that's a mismatch, and Google notices.

Landing page experience

This looks at what happens after the click. Does the page match what the ad promised? Is it easy to use? Does it load quickly? A slow, irrelevant, or hard to navigate page drags this score down, even if your ad copy is great.

The Ad Rank formula, and what it means for you

Google decides ad position using something called Ad Rank. In simple terms, it's your bid multiplied by your Quality Score, plus a few smaller factors like ad extensions.

What this means in practice: a competitor with a Quality Score of 8 can outrank you, and pay less per click than you, even if your bid is higher. Quality Score is the multiplier that decides how far your budget actually goes.

Quick check: Go to Google Ads, add the Quality Score columns to your keyword view, and sort by score. If your top spending keywords sit below 5, that's costing you money right now.

Quality Score benchmarks, 1 to 10

ScoreWhat it usually means
1 to 3Poor. Ad and keyword are mismatched, or the landing page isn't relevant. Expect high costs and weak positions.
4 to 5Below average. Some elements are working, but something, usually relevance or landing page, is holding you back.
6 to 7Average to good. The core elements are working together reasonably well.
8 to 9Strong. Ad, keyword, and landing page are closely aligned. You're likely paying less than competitors for the same position.
10Excellent. Rare in practice, and usually only seen on very tightly targeted, well optimised campaigns.

Five ways to improve it

1. Tighten your ad group structure

The single biggest issue in most accounts is ad groups with too many loosely related keywords. If one ad group contains ten different keywords covering different services or intents, no single ad can be relevant to all of them. Break ad groups down by specific theme, ideally down to a handful of closely related keywords each.

2. Write copy for the keyword, not the account

Once your ad groups are tight, write ad copy that speaks directly to those specific keywords. If someone searches "boiler repair same day," your headline should reflect that urgency and service, not a generic line about your company.

3. Match your landing page to the ad

If your ad promises same day boiler repair, the landing page should be about same day boiler repair, not your homepage or a general services page. The closer the match, the better the experience, and the better the score.

4. Fix page speed

A slow landing page hurts your Quality Score and your conversion rate at the same time. If your page takes more than a few seconds to load, especially on mobile, that's worth fixing before anything else.

5. Cut keywords that are dragging you down

Sometimes a keyword just isn't going to perform well, no matter what you do to the ad or landing page. If a keyword has a consistently low Quality Score and isn't converting, removing it can improve the overall health of the account, even though it feels like giving something up.

What Quality Score doesn't tell you

Quality Score is a useful signal, but it's not the full picture. It won't tell you if a keyword is actually driving revenue. It won't tell you if your targeting is reaching the right audience. And a high Quality Score doesn't guarantee conversions, it just means your ad is efficient at getting relevant clicks.

Think of it as one input into a bigger decision, not the scoreboard for whether a campaign is working.

The bottom line

Quality Score rewards accounts that are built around relevance, tight ad groups, specific copy, and landing pages that match what was promised. It's not a vanity metric. It's one of the clearest levers you have for paying less and ranking higher at the same time.

If you haven't checked the Quality Score breakdown on your top keywords recently, that's a good place to start.
