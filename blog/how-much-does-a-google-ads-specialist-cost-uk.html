How Much Does a Google Ads Specialist Cost in the UK? (2026)

Freelancer, agency or in-house. What does Google Ads management actually cost in the UK right now? Here's an honest breakdown with real numbers, no vague ranges and no agenda beyond giving you a clear answer.

Why pricing is so confusing

Ask three Google Ads specialists what they charge and you'll get three completely different answers. Sometimes they vary by thousands of pounds a month. That's not because one is ripping you off and two aren't. It's because "Google Ads management" means very different things depending on who's doing it, what's included and how they structure their fees.

This post breaks down the main options. Freelancer, agency and in-house. What each typically costs in 2026 and what you should actually be getting for your money.

The three main options

1. Google Ads freelancer

A freelancer working independently, usually with a small number of clients. You deal directly with the person doing the work. No account managers, no passing jobs down to a junior who's been in the role for six months.

Typical UK pricing in 2026:

StructureTypical rangeWhat's usually included
Hourly rate£40 to £120/hrAd hoc work, optimisations, consultancy
Day rate£300 to £600/dayIntensive work, builds, restructures
Monthly retainer£400 to £2,000/moOngoing management, reporting, strategy
One-off project£500 to £2,500Account builds, audits, restructures

The wide range reflects experience level, location and what's actually included. A junior freelancer charging £40 per hour and a senior specialist charging £100 per hour are not delivering the same thing. Experience matters more in Google Ads than in most disciplines because bad decisions compound quickly when real budget is involved.

2. Google Ads agency

An agency brings a team. Usually a dedicated account manager, a specialist actually running the campaigns and sometimes a strategist involved at a higher level. More resource, more process and naturally, more cost.

Typical UK agency pricing in 2026:

StructureTypical rangeNotes
Monthly retainer£1,000 to £5,000+/moMost common structure
% of ad spend10 to 20% of spendCommon addition to retainer
Setup fee£500 to £2,000Often charged on top of first month
Minimum contract3 to 12 monthsUsually required

The percentage of spend model is worth looking at carefully. If an agency takes 15% of your ad spend, they earn more as your spend increases. That doesn't always align with what's best for your account. You want lower spend and better results. They benefit from higher spend regardless of results.

3. In-house specialist

Hiring someone to manage Google Ads as part of their day-to-day role. This only really makes sense at significant scale, typically when you're spending upwards of £20,000 per month on Google Ads and need dedicated daily attention on the account.

Typical UK salary in 2026:

LevelSalary rangeNotes
Junior PPC executive£22,000 to £30,0001 to 2 years experience

Add employer NI, pension, holiday, equipment and management overhead and a mid-level hire costs somewhere between £40,000 and £55,000 all-in. For most businesses spending under £15,000 per month on ads, a freelancer or agency will deliver better value.

What should be included in Google Ads management?

Regardless of who you hire, the following should come as standard. Not as extras, not as add-ons and not as things you have to ask for.

  • Regular account optimisation covering keyword management, bid adjustments, negative keywords and ad testing
  • Search term analysis so you know what your ads are actually showing for and wasted spend gets cut
  • Performance reporting that is clear, regular and explains what's happening and why
  • Conversion tracking because if your specialist can't tell you how many leads or sales the campaigns are generating, something is wrong
  • Direct communication meaning access to the person actually doing the work, not just an account manager relaying messages

Red flags to watch for

Not all Google Ads management is equal. These are the warning signs worth taking seriously before you sign anything.

  • Percentage of spend fees without a cap create a conflict of interest. You want lower spend and better results. They earn more if spend goes up.
  • Long minimum contracts of 12 months or more suggest a lack of confidence in results. A good specialist should be willing to work on shorter terms.
  • Vague reporting is a red flag. "Impressions went up" is not a meaningful metric. If they can't tell you your cost per lead, something is being hidden.
  • No ownership of your account is a serious problem. Your Google Ads account should always be in your name. Some agencies hold accounts in theirs, which means you lose access to everything if you ever decide to leave.
  • No mention of conversion tracking before starting means they're not planning to measure what actually matters.

Freelancer vs agency — which is right for you?

The honest answer depends on your situation and your monthly spend level.

  • If you're spending under £5,000 per month on ads, a freelancer almost always delivers better value. You're not spending enough to justify agency overheads.
  • If you're spending between £5,000 and £20,000 per month, either can work well. The quality of the individual matters far more than the structure. A strong freelancer will consistently outperform a mediocre agency at this level.
  • If you're spending over £20,000 per month, an agency's team resource starts to make sense, or a senior in-house hire becomes worth considering.

The most important question isn't freelancer or agency. It's who is actually doing the work. In many agencies your account is managed by a junior executive who has been in the role for 18 months. A freelancer with 15 years of experience will consistently outperform that regardless of what the agency charges.

How to evaluate a Google Ads specialist before hiring

Before committing to anyone, ask these five questions and pay close attention to how they answer.

  1. Who will actually manage my account day to day? At an agency, this is often not the person you meet in the pitch.
  2. Can you show me examples of results you have achieved? Push for specifics including cost per lead, conversion rate improvements and ROAS figures.
  3. How do you charge and is there a minimum contract? Fixed fee or transparent hourly pricing is a good sign. Vague retainer structures with no clear scope are not.
  4. Will my account be in my name? This is non-negotiable. If the answer is no, walk away.
  5. How will you measure success? If the answer isn't tied directly to leads, sales or revenue, be cautious.

The bottom line

For a UK business spending between £1,000 and £10,000 per month on Google Ads, a freelance specialist charging £60 to £100 per hour or £400 to £600 per day is typically the best value option in 2026. You get direct access to an experienced person, lower overheads than an agency and no minimum contract keeping you locked in if results aren't there.
