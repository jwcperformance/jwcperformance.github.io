How Long Does It Take for Google Ads to Work?

This is probably the question I get asked most often, either before someone starts running ads or after the first few weeks when results aren't where they expected. The honest answer is that it depends on several things, but there are some clear patterns worth knowing about.

The short answer

You can get clicks and even leads within the first few days of a campaign going live. But getting consistent, predictable results from Google Ads typically takes between 3 and 6 months. That's not a cop-out answer. It reflects how the platform actually works.

Google's algorithms need data to optimise effectively. The bidding systems, quality scores and audience signals that determine when and where your ads appear all improve as the account learns. You can't shortcut that learning period, but you can manage it well or poorly.

What happens in the first 30 days

The first month is primarily a data-gathering period. Your ads go live, clicks come in and you start to see which keywords are generating interest and which aren't. Conversion rates in the first month are often lower than they will be later because the account hasn't yet learned which searches, audiences and times of day perform best.

This is also when the most wasted spend tends to happen if the account isn't managed carefully. Broad match keywords can pull in irrelevant traffic. Search terms need reviewing regularly and negative keywords need adding to cut out the searches that will never convert.

A new account in its first month should not be judged on cost per lead alone. The more useful question is whether the right people are clicking the ads, whether the tracking is working and whether the search terms showing up make sense for the business.

Important: If conversion tracking isn't set up correctly before the campaign launches, the first month's data is essentially useless for optimisation purposes. Tracking should always be confirmed and tested before any money is spent.

A realistic timeline

Week 1 to 2

Campaigns live, data begins

Ads are approved and running. First clicks come in. Search terms report starts building. Initial conversion data appears if tracking is set up correctly. Focus should be on checking search terms daily and adding negatives for anything irrelevant.

Week 2 to 4

First optimisations

Enough data to start making meaningful changes. Underperforming keywords paused or adjusted. Ad copy tested. Bid adjustments made for device, location and time of day based on early performance signals. Cost per lead is usually higher than it will become.

Month 2 to 3

Performance stabilises

The account has enough data to identify patterns. Conversion rates start to settle. Smart bidding strategies become viable if enough conversions have been recorded. The most significant cost per lead improvements typically happen in this period as the account is refined based on real data.

Month 3 to 6

Consistent results

A well-managed account should be producing predictable, consistent results by this point. Quality scores improve as relevance increases. Automated bidding strategies work more effectively with more conversion data. The focus shifts from fixing problems to scaling what works.

Month 6 onwards

Compounding improvements

Accounts that have been managed well for 6 months or more often outperform newer accounts significantly, even at the same budget level. The historical data, quality scores and audience signals built up over time give the account advantages that a newer account simply doesn't have.

What affects how quickly you see results

Your budget

A higher budget means more clicks, which means more data, which means faster learning. An account spending £5,000 per month will collect enough conversion data in 4 to 6 weeks to run effective automated bidding. An account spending £300 per month might take 4 to 6 months to collect the same amount of data.

This doesn't mean you need a large budget to get results. It just means your expectations around timeline should be calibrated to your spend level.

How competitive your market is

In highly competitive markets where multiple advertisers are bidding aggressively, costs per click are higher and it takes longer to find the profitable pockets of traffic. In less competitive markets, results often come faster and at lower cost.

How good your landing page is

Google Ads gets people to click. Your landing page gets them to convert. An account with excellent campaigns sending traffic to a poor landing page will always underperform. If conversion rates are low despite good click-through rates, the landing page is usually where to look first.

Whether conversion tracking is set up correctly

I keep coming back to this because it matters that much. An account without accurate conversion tracking cannot be optimised effectively. You are essentially flying blind. The algorithm cannot learn what a good conversion looks like if it isn't told when one happens.

How well the account is managed

An actively managed account will reach consistent performance significantly faster than one that is set up and left to run. Regular search term reviews, negative keyword additions, bid adjustments and ad testing all compound over time. An unmanaged account tends to drift towards worse performance as the market changes around it.

Why some people give up too soon

The most common mistake I see is businesses stopping Google Ads after 4 to 6 weeks because results weren't good enough. In most cases, that's exactly when the account was starting to gather enough data to improve. Stopping at that point means you never find out what it could have delivered.

That doesn't mean you should keep spending money on something that genuinely isn't working. But low initial performance in a well-set-up account is normal, not a sign that Google Ads doesn't work for your business.

Google Ads vs SEO — a note on expectations

Google Ads can deliver clicks within hours of launching. SEO takes months or years to build. That's often why businesses turn to paid search first. But Google Ads isn't instant either, at least not in terms of consistent, cost-effective results.

The most effective approach for most businesses is to run both in parallel. Ads provide immediate visibility and data while SEO builds up. The data from your ads campaigns can also inform your SEO strategy by showing you which keywords are actually driving conversions.

The bottom line

Give a new Google Ads account at least 3 months before making a final judgement on whether it works for your business. The first month is about gathering data, the second is about finding what works, and the third is when things typically start to click into place.

If you are three months in and still not seeing results, the issue is more likely with the setup or management than with the platform itself. That's where a fresh audit of the account can be useful.
